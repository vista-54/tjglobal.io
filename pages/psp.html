According to the legislation of the UK e-money represents a monetary value, represented by the liabilities of the Issuer, which is stored in electronic form and is accepted for payments by third parties. Electronic money is considered to be the electronic equivalent of coins and banknotes intended for making payments for small amounts.

In the UK, the payment system, whose functions include the issuance of electronic money, as well as in Europe, can only be created in the form of EMI (Electronic Money Institution). Depending on the turnover, there are 2 types of electronic money companies in the UK: EMI, a classic electronic money institution, in which case the fee (duty) in FCA will be £5,000; Small EM (if the monthly turnover does not exceed €3 000 000, if the company did not operate, then we estimate the forecasts), in this case, the contribution to the FCA will be £1 000. It is also worth noting that a small electronic money institution (EMI) has the right to provide its services only within the UK. If the limit of €3 000 000 is reached, the applicant is obliged to give in to receive a full EMI license from a small one

REGULATION OF ELECTRONIC MONEY ISSUER

As with the European financial market, EMI's operations in the UK are governed by the EU Directive 2009/110/EC on Electronic money (hereinafter referred to as the EU Directive). In addition, at the state level, the Inspectorate for supervision of financial institutions of the United Kingdom (FCA) issued a Regulation on electronic money (hereinafter - the Regulation). This Provision was approved on the basis of an EU Directive, but it contains more details regarding the establishment of an EMI within the United Kingdom.

Therefore, the Central regulator issuing licenses to institutions of electronic money in the UK is the Inspectorate for control over the activities of financial organizations in great Britain (hereinafter - FCA). FCA regulates the use of electronic money in accordance with the EU Directive on electronic money. Issuers of electronic money also have obligations in accordance with the Provisions on money laundering. If suspicious activity is detected, the company is legally obliged to report it to the authorities.

The financial supervision service requires issuers of electronic money to prove that they are able to properly control the risks they face in their work. Measures that may be taken by electronic money issuers to fulfil their legal obligations are discussed in the Guidelines of the Joint steering group on money laundering control.

Simultaneously with the application for a license, the Regulator will need to submit a detailed business plan; a detailed description of the procedures for identifying customers, preventing money laundering, obtained illegally; a detailed description of the operations that are planned to be provided to customers; full information about the founders/shareholders, as well as about the top management of the company.

The total term of consideration of the application for a license is up to 3 months. However, if the FCA deems it necessary to request additional information on the applicant, the period of consideration of the application may be extended to 12 months. REQUIREMENTS FOR THE ESTABLISHMENT OF ELECTRONIC MONEY IN THE UK.

To apply for a license for electronic money (EMI) in the UK can only tax resident companies. This means the applicant company can only be registered in England. In turn, the founders / shareholders of such companies can be both residents and non-residents of the United Kingdom, individuals or legal entities.

UK law, unlike many other jurisdictions, contains quite strict requirements for the top management of the company electronic money (EMI), among which we can highlight the need to have sufficient education and knowledge for the management of the Issuer of electronic money. This implies the following:

- top management should have experience in launching such companies. For FCA, this correspondence is important, people who do not have any experience in this field, usually do not get permission.

- management of e-money company should be from the UK. The FCA identifies the following key points:

1. The company must have more UK resident Directors (or an equal number) than foreign Directors (their nationality does not matter).

2. The FCA implies that the resident Director's decisions have a dominant power, he can say "no" to the policies, strategies and decisions that usually come from abroad, in case of their conflict with UK law or regulations.

3. Meetings of the Board of Directors should be held in the UK.

4. Operational day-to-day decisions should be made in the UK and the office itself should be based in the same state.

5. The company may have a non-resident Director on the Board of Directors of a British company if the rest of the company is based in the UK.

6. The CEO must be a resident and reside in the UK, and usually in practice, a compliance Manager too.

7. The anti-money laundering officer, who may also be the Director General, if qualified) must be a resident of the United Kingdom.

8. If someone moves to the UK to be CEO, he should read the rules of management, depending on the business model, therefore you may need to recruit the local Manager which has the authority to monitor compliance.
